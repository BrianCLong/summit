# AI-IO Watchlist (2026 H1)

## Overview
This watchlist tracks public AI-enabled influence operations (AI-IO) threat programs, bot infrastructure trends, and emerging detection methods. It serves as a deterministic reference for the IntelGraph platform.

## A. Most important AI-IO research / capability programs

- **P1: IARPA HIATUS**
  - **Why now**: It is directly aimed at authorship attribution, author privacy, and machine-generated text detection, with explicit relevance to malicious information campaigns. For Summit, this is the cleanest public signal that stylometry-plus-explainability will matter again, now in multilingual and human/machine-mixed environments.
  - **Observable signals**: New datasets in English, Arabic, Chinese, Russian; explainable authorship methods; machine-generated text fingerprinting that survives paraphrase; benchmarks usable for cross-language attribution.
  - **Summit fit**: Multilingual narrative clustering, authorship residuals.

- **P1: DARPA ICS (Intrinsic Cognitive Security)**
  - **Why now**: While framed around mixed reality, it is one of the clearest official "cognitive attack" programs. The important Summit takeaway is formalization: defenders will want proofs or bounded guarantees about manipulative interface behavior, not only heuristic moderation.
  - **Observable signals**: Formal cognitive-attack threat models; measurable operator-deception scenarios; machine-verifiable assurance cases for human-facing systems.
  - **Summit fit**: Trust-network impact scoring, formal verification of UI claims.

- **P1: IARPA human-systems portfolio around social influence / decision-making**
  - **Why now**: IARPA’s 2026 activity and program-management focus includes social influence, behavior change, and decision-making. Even where the specific program names are broader than online disinfo, the research direction lines up with campaign-effect modeling rather than just content filtering.
  - **Observable signals**: DECIPHER / LocUS / related releases; decision-support methods for sparse, ambiguous environments; effect-measurement programs, not just detection programs.
  - **Summit fit**: Campaign effect modeling, narrative propagation graphs.

- **P2: Platform/provider threat-intelligence programs as quasi-research pipelines**
  - **Why now**: OpenAI, Google, and Meta are now functionally part of the research surface because they see misuse telemetry others do not. They are repeatedly documenting covert influence operations, government-backed AI misuse, and coordinated inauthentic behavior patterns.
  - **Observable signals**: New quarterly misuse typologies; traces of model-to-platform handoff; repeated TTPs across vendors.
  - **Summit fit**: Threat intelligence integration.

## B. State-sponsored bot infrastructure trends

- **P1: "Living off the land" influence infrastructure**
  - **Why now**: Expectation: more operations will ride on trusted SaaS, enterprise tooling, cloud identities, and legitimate collaboration channels instead of bespoke malware-only stacks. This is already showing up in state-backed cyber reporting and matters because it blurs the line between cyber intrusion and influence staging.
  - **Observable signals**: Repeated tenant / provider reuse across otherwise distinct accounts; abuse of productivity suites, CDNs, cloud storage, or contractor tooling; narrative bursts shortly after credential exposure or account takeover events.
  - **Summit fit**: Identity collision heuristics, infrastructure reuse fingerprints.

- **P1: Synthetic identity + rented-account operations**
  - **Why now**: Expectation: more campaigns will use AI-enhanced personas, bought or rented accounts, and blended human/bot posting to avoid old bot heuristics. Cloudflare’s recent reporting and broader 2025–26 platform reports point in this direction.
  - **Observable signals**: Impossible travel; abrupt topic pivots on aged accounts; style drift with preserved account social graph; synchronized "legit-looking" engagement rings.
  - **Summit fit**: Synthetic identity detection, account-role inference.

- **P1: Multilingual narrative laundering**
  - **Why now**: Expectation: operators will seed in one language, repackage in another, and re-import narratives with localized style. This follows directly from the growing use of AI for multilingual output and from HIATUS-style authorship problems.
  - **Observable signals**: Semantically equivalent claims crossing languages with low lexical overlap; common citation skeletons and rhetorical templates; time-lagged cascades from fringe channels to mainstream platforms.
  - **Summit fit**: Cross-language narrative-family clustering.

- **P2: Cyber + influence coupling as default, not exception**
  - **Why now**: Expectation: intrusion, credential theft, and influence amplification will increasingly share infrastructure and timing windows. Google’s reporting on full-lifecycle AI use and longstanding APT44 integration patterns support this direction.
  - **Observable signals**: Correlation between intrusion telemetry and narrative escalation; same victim set appearing in both phishing and influence datasets; campaign pivots after sanctions, elections, or kinetic events.
  - **Summit fit**: Hybrid event correlation.

## C. Emerging detection methods: graph + LLM hybrid models

- **P1: Graph foundation models for IO-driver discovery**
  - **Why now**: IOHunter is squarely on Summit’s target line: uncovering the accounts that orchestrate campaigns, not merely classifying content.
  - **Observable signals**: Prioritize account-role inference; surface driver / broker / amplifier nodes; retain temporal edge semantics.
  - **Summit fit**: Narrative graph engine, coordination detector.

- **P1: Topology + language-embedding fusion**
  - **Why now**: Aletheia explicitly combines topological features with language embeddings for detection and forecasting. This is close to the practical near-term architecture Summit should test first.
  - **Observable signals**: Keep graph features first-class; use LLMs for embedding / explanation / weak labeling; do not let pure text classification dominate campaign scoring.
  - **Summit fit**: Graph+LLM detection methods.

- **P1: Text-attributed graph benchmarks for misinformation**
  - **Why now**: TAGFN and TAG-AD indicate the benchmark ecosystem is finally catching up to graph+LLM detection for fake news and misinformation. That means Summit can anchor eval work to public artifacts instead of bespoke internal demos.
  - **Observable signals**: Add benchmark adapters early; require out-of-domain generalization tests; track calibration, not just F1.
  - **Summit fit**: Benchmark integration.

- **P2: LLM-assisted influence-campaign classification**
  - **Why now**: There is growing work on using LLMs directly for campaign detection, often with metadata and network features. Useful, but secondary: the risk is brittle overfitting and poor transfer if the graph substrate is weak.
  - **Observable signals**: Use LLMs as triage, explanation, and labeling assistants; keep final detection grounded in propagation, account behavior, and infra reuse.
  - **Summit fit**: LLM assistants.

- **P2: Evidence-grounded multimodal graph methods**
  - **Why now**: Multimodal misinformation work is converging on graph-based evidence consistency, which matters for image-caption, video-caption, and narrative-package detection.
  - **Observable signals**: Plan for image/video nodes in the graph; add "claim package" objects, not just posts.
  - **Summit fit**: Multimodal graph elements.
